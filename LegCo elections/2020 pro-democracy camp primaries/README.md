# Introduction

Date(s) held: July 11 and 12，2020

Purpose: to pick candidates for the subsequently-postponed 7th Legislative Council (LegCo) elections held in September to maximize pro-democracy camp's chance for obtaining a majority in the Council (also known as the "35+" project) for the first time

Electoral system: SNTV (geographical constituencies and the District Council (2nd) functional constituency); FPTP (Health Services functional constituency) [1]

# Overwiev

Riding a wave of disaffection with the government over the since-retracted extradition bill and its inability to implement the so-called "five demands" [2] by the protesters, pro-democracy candidates collectively won a historic 389 out of 452 popularly elected seats in the November 2019 local elections - the biggest landslide in the city's electoral history. 

However, upon assuming office, the democrats quickly found themselves making little progress inside the political system. District Councils in Hong Kong, unlike its counterparts in most of the world, were merely advisoral where the government-appointed District Officers and the Home Affairs Department has the final say in the decision-making proccess. Government officals has become increasingly uncooperative when it came to non-local issues; officials and civil servants often staged walkouts when the District Councils passed motions to advance any political issue or condemn police violence, claiming they were bringing up topics "unrelated to their role and function". 

Meanwhile, street protests have largely died down following the outbreak of COVID-19 (or some like to say, the Wuhan pneumonia) and overall fatigue of the general movement, one needed to find a way to keep the resistance going. A new objective was quickly set: to win a majority of seats in the city's Legislative Council, which was at least not an advisory body.

Enter Professor Benny Tai Yiu-ting 戴耀廷, an associate law professor of the University of Hong Kong. Best known as one of the initiators of the Occupy Central protests in 2014, he came up with a way to force the government to get the "five demands" done which he called the "10 steps of burn with us [3]" doctrine: by electing more than 35 pro-democracy members to the Legislative Council, the newly-elected legislators would indiscriminately veto the government's annual budget and any other bill until said demands were implemented by the officials. Tai also envisioned a constitutional crisis so that Chief Executive Carrie Lam would be forced to resign when it was all said and done. But therein lied the problem: who should the 35 legislators be? 

Tai immediately suggested a primary election to prevent infighting within the pro-democracy camp and to maximize the number of pro-democracy candidates getting into the LegCo. The "infighting" was a by-product of the pro-democracy camp's split that occured in the 2010s: in June 2010, the Democratic Party (DP) and ADPL, representing the moderate factions of the camp, teamed up with the government and pro-Beijing parties to pass a reform package that still lacked a concrete plan to universal suffrage of the CE and LegCo. Months before the vote, leaders of both parties, as well as moderate scholars, negotiated with Chinese Central Government officials in the Liaison Office building to discuss about the package and hoped to get a better deal from them - much to the anger of the hardliners, namely the League of Social Democrats and HKGolden netizens. While genuine universal suffrage was never on the table, the central government reluctantly green-lighted DP's proposal of expanding the franchise of the new district council funcational constituency (FC) so that every voter who wasn't registered to the existing FCs, instead of just the councilors themselves, could return 5 district councilors to LegCo. 

As a result, new political groups, such as Neo Democrats, People Power (PP) and Civic Passion (breakaway groups of the DP, LSD and PP respectively) have been formed, hoping to tilt the needle to the more confrontational side when it came to the Hong Kong and Chinese governments. The infighting within the camp cost them much-needed seats in the 2012 LegCo elections: in the Kowloon East (KLE) and New Territories West constituencies, despite receiving a majority of the vote, pro-democracy candidates only won a minority of the seats by simply running too much candidates. Take the KLE constituency for example: in 2012, when the pro-Beijing camp only fielded 3 lists to the 5-seat KLE, pro-democrats had six; Things were about the same in 2016, with the anti-establishment camp running a staggering 8 lists. Tai and the pro-democrats saw this and vowed not to let this happen again.
