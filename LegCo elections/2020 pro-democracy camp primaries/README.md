# Introduction

Date(s) held: July 11 and 12, 2020

Purpose: to pick candidates for the subsequently-postponed 7th Legislative Council (LegCo) elections held in September to maximize pro-democracy camp's chance for obtaining a majority in the Council (also known as the "35+" project) for the first time

Electoral system: SNTV (geographical constituencies and the District Council (2nd) functional constituency); FPTP (Health Services functional constituency) [1]

# Overview

Riding a wave of disaffection with the government over the since-retracted extradition bill and its inability to implement the so-called "five demands" [2] by the protesters, pro-democracy candidates collectively won a historic 389 out of 452 popularly elected seats in the November 2019 local elections - the biggest landslide in the city's electoral history.

However, upon assuming office, the democrats quickly found themselves making little progress inside the political system. District Councils in Hong Kong, unlike its counterparts in most of the world, were merely advisory where the government-appointed District Officers and the Home Affairs Department had the final say in the decision-making process. Government officials had become increasingly uncooperative when it came to non-local issues; officials and civil servants often staged walkouts when the District Councils passed motions to advance any political issue or condemn police violence, claiming they were bringing up topics "unrelated to their role and function".

Meanwhile, street protests have largely died down following the outbreak of COVID-19 (or some like to say, the Wuhan pneumonia) and overall fatigue of the general movement, one needed to find a way to keep the resistance going. A new objective was quickly set: to win a majority of seats in the city's Legislative Council, which was at least not an advisory body.

Enter Professor Benny Tai Yiu-ting, an associate law professor of the University of Hong Kong. Best known as one of the initiators of the Occupy Central protests in 2014, he came up with a way to force the government to get the "five demands" done which he called the "10 steps of burn with us [3]" doctrine: by electing more than 35 pro-democracy members to the Legislative Council, the newly-elected legislators would indiscriminately veto the government's annual budget and any other bill until said demands were implemented by the officials. Tai also envisioned a constitutional crisis so that Chief Executive Carrie Lam would be forced to resign when it was all said and done. But therein lies the problem: who should the 35 legislators be?

Tai immediately suggested a primary election to prevent infighting within the pro-democracy camp and to maximize the number of pro-democracy candidates getting into the LegCo. The "infighting" was a by-product of the pro-democracy camp's split that occurred in the 2010s: in June 2010, the Democratic Party (DP) and ADPL, representing the moderate factions of the camp, teamed up with the government and pro-Beijing parties to pass a reform package that still lacked a concrete plan to universal suffrage of the CE and LegCo. Months before the vote, leaders of both parties, as well as moderate scholars, negotiated with Chinese Central Government officials in the Liaison Office building to discuss about the package and hoped to get a better deal from them - much to the anger of the hardliners, namely the League of Social Democrats and HKGolden netizens. While genuine universal suffrage was never on the table, the central government reluctantly green-lighted DP's proposal of expanding the franchise of the new district council functional constituency (DC2 FC) so that every voter who wasn't registered to the existing FCs, instead of just the councilors themselves, could return 5 district councilors to LegCo.

As a result, new political groups, such as Neo Democrats, People Power (PP) and Civic Passion (breakaway groups of the DP, LSD and PP respectively) have been formed, hoping to tilt the needle to the more confrontational side when it comes to the Hong Kong and Chinese governments. The infighting within the camp cost them much-needed seats in the 2012 LegCo elections: in the Kowloon East (KLE) and New Territories West (NTW) constituencies, despite receiving a majority of the vote, pro-democracy candidates only won a minority of the seats by simply running too many candidates. Take the KLE constituency for example: in 2012, when the pro-Beijing camp only fielded 3 lists to the 5-seat KLE, pro-democrats had six; Things were about the same in 2016, with the anti-establishment camp running a staggering 8 lists. Tai and the pro-democrats saw this and vowed not to let this happen again.

For the pro-democrats, getting a majority in LegCo was already a monumental task; with the spread of COVID-19 already slowing down street resistance, Beijing’s suppressions have only become more aggravated. In May 2020, in an unprecedented move, the Chinese National People’s Congress (NPC) announced it would draft a national security law  (NSL) targeting secession, terrorism, collusion and subversion against the central government - all without any input or consultation from Hong Kong’s political institutions. Unsurprisingly, the legislation received unanimous support from the rubber-stamp NPC on June 30, 2020 and became law the same day. After the enactment, Hong Kong officials were quick to denounce the primaries, alleging the legislator-hopefuls could violate the new NSL if they voted against the budget, to which Tai replied his work was in accordance with the Basic Law and downplayed any claims of wrongdoings.

# Contesting parties and non-participants

Most parties and groups within the pro-democracy faction (including localists groups and traditional parties currently/formerly represented in the LegCo) participated in the primaries, and they were as follows:
(Note: This list is not all inclusive of every participating party)

Traditional parties:

The Democratic Party 民主黨
  
Emphasizing dialogue with the mainland, the Hong Kong government and nonviolence resistance, it was the flagship opposition party in the city which counted some of the city’s household political figures. For years, the party was criticized for being too moderate and ineffectual by the radical democrats and localists. 
The party had 7 legislators (5 from GCs and 2 from DC2) by the time of the primaries, all of whom were running for re-election.

Civic Party 公民黨

Created in 2006 by like-minded legal practitioners and academics, the party was often nicknamed the “Barristers’ Party”. It was known for representing the middle class and the professionals. Unlike the Democratic Party, it has attempted to adopt limited nativist positions following the rise of localism. 
With 6 legislators (4 from GCs and 2 from FCs) as of the primaries, the party would field 4 lists for the primaries with the slogan “越打壓越頑強”.

Hong Kong Association for Democracy and People's Livelihood (ADPL) 香港民主民生協進會

Established in 1986, the ADPL was seen as an old-guard party which had a strong basis in Sham Shui Po. Politically wise, it was viewed as more moderate and anti-confrontational than the DP due to its participation in the transitional-period Provisional Legislative Council which most of the democrats boycotted.
Following the defeat of long-time legislator Frederick Fung in 2016, the party did not have any seats in the LegCo by the time of the primaries. The group announced it would field 2 young candidates for the primaries, one in each of the Kowloon constituencies.

Labour Party 工黨

Created in 2011 by three veteran legislators, the party emphasized pro-labour stances such as the implementation of a universal retirement protection scheme and anti-monopoly laws. Dozens of like-minded trade unions were also affiliated with the party. By the time of the primaries, it only had one LegCo representative in Fernando Cheung, who was retiring. The party then announced it would only run one candidate for the primaries (not in Cheung’s constituency).

Neo Democrats 新民主同盟

The group was formed in 2010 by former members of the Democratic Party’s Reformist faction who left the party en masse following the DP’s support of the 2010 reform package. Viewed as the “pragmatic localist” alternative in its early days, the group distanced itself from the more radical counterparts following the Umbrella Movement and was seen as a traditional, mainstream party nowadays. Gary Fan, representing NTE, was the group’s sole legislator as of July 2020; for the primaries, the group finalized on 2 lists: one in NTE, and one in NTW.

League of Social Democrats 社會民主連線

Founded in 2006, the group stressed on “street actions” and “parliamentary struggles" and branded itself as the “unequivocal opposition”, unconventional at its times. It was also the first political group inside the LegCo to extensively adopt filibuster tactics and put up theatrics inside the chamber. The party had no representation inside LegCo as of the primaries; for the primaries, it decided on 2 candidates, one in KLW and one in NTE.

People Power 人民力量

A breakaway group of the LSD, it was created in 2011 by two LegCo members following its disagreements with the LSD on whatever to “punish” the Democratic Party and ADPL for the upcoming 2011 local elections. The two groups, however, re-established relations in 2013 following Wong Yuk-man’s departure from the party and shift to nativism. The group had one member (Raymond Chan, NTE) inside the LegCo, who would run for another term; besides Chan, his long-time radio show partner Tam Tak-chi would also be running in KLE.

Non-traditional parties who were not part of the localists:

Kickstart Wan Chai 灣仔起步

It was created in 2019 by Wan Chai district councilor Clarisse Yeung as a coordination platform for first-time pro-democracy candidates preparing to run in that year’s local elections. Yeung was a candidate for the primaries running in the HKI constituency.

Chai Wan Startup 柴灣起動

Same concept as Kickstart Wan Chai, except it was founded by Eastern district councilor Andy Chui and had a focus in the Chai Wan/Siu Sai Wan areas. Like Yeung, he was also a primary candidate for the HKI constituency.

Localist groups

(Note: localist groups and individuals were often lumped together into a wider spectrum of “pro-democracy camp” by the media, and many localists disapproved of that. Here, “localist” refers to a combination of self-determination groups (or “left-wing localists” according to academia), district-based groups that sprung after the Umbrella Movement or the anti-extradition protests, and individuals who have expressed nativist views. The “right-wing localists”, in the academic sense, did not run in the primaries as they believed that they had been either excluded by the political system that there was no point in running, or they despised everyone who was part of the broader “pro-democracy camp”.)

Team Chu Hoi-dick of New Territories West 朱凱廸新西團隊

Its founder, Eddie Chu, was the sole localist member of the LegCo. Elected in 2016 under a “self-determination” and Heung Yee Kuk [4]-reform platform as a political newcomer, he maintained good relations with his like-minded partners and traditional parties before the former were all disqualified over the oath-taking controversy [5]. Due to his political views, he was once barred from running in the February 2019 village head elections, but was permitted to run in November‘s local council elections, although he did not win a seat. He was running in NTW in the primaries. 

Tin Shui Wai Connection 天水連線

Inspired by the 2019 protests, it was created in 2019 by several LIHKG netizens living in Tin Shui Wai aiming to prevent incumbent pro-Beijing district councilors from winning uncontested. Holding 4 seats in the Yuen Long District Council, one of the group’s long-term goals was to separate Tin Shui Wai from Yuen Long District. The group fielded a single ticket in NTW for the primaries.

Tuen Mun Community Network 屯門社區網絡

The group was formed in 2015 after the failure of the Umbrella protests, and has cultivated a following of Tuen Mun residents thanks to their focus on local issues such as once exposing an elevator corruption scandal. It was also part of the Community Network Union, a political alliance of six community groups holding localist views. For the primaries, the group slated district councilor Sam Cheung to run in NTW.

Sha Tin Community Network 沙田社區網絡

Founded by Ventus Lau in 2015. Originally a member of Neo Democrats, Lau quit the group after the Umbrella protests following clashes with Gary Fan over the disaffiliation of the CUHK Students' Union from the Hong Kong Federation of Students (HKFS) [5], which he viewed the HKFS a burden on democratic development. He was also a candidate for the 2018 NTE by-election, but was promptly disqualified because of his pro-independence views.
He decided to run for LegCo one more time, participating in the primaries under the “Localist” banner.

Non-participating parties: (T=traditional parties; L=localists)

Neighbourhood and Worker's Service Centre 街坊工友服務處 (T)

Despite not being its chairman, the group‘s most notable member has been legislator Leung Yiu-chung, who has been heavily criticized for not presiding the LegCo presidential election in 2016, instead voluntarily giving up power to the pro-Beijing camp “without a fight”. Originally planning to participate in the primaries, Leung did not rule out withdrawing even if he lost. This caused outcry among the pro-democrats, with localists like TSWC vowing to boycott the primaries if Leung decided to run at all.

North District Blueprint 北區連線 (L)

It was formed in 2019 by residents living in the city’s North District in preparation of the district council elections held that year. The group won 7 seats in that year’s election, becoming the largest political group in the North District Council (NDC). However, the group has occasionally clashed with traditional democrats and the primaries’ organizers (mainly Benny Tai) during the primary period.

For instance, group member Hendrick Lui, who was planning to run in NTE, slammed the primaries as “just another managed big tent” controlled by the democratic establishment, and questioned the fairness of the primaries. He also clashed with Benny Tai on whether to adopt a “common political platform” for the primaries; Tai worried the signing of said platform would cause mass disqualifications, which Lui criticized as "an act of practicing self-censorship”. 

Another potential candidate, Law Ting-fai (brother of NDC chairman Jasper Law), stated that he would also not be participating in the primaries due to the NWSC’s participation, also having the same doubts as Lui. 
Law instead opted for the general election, while Lui eventually joined the primary as an independent.

Civic Passion 熱血公民 (L)

Founded in 2012, the group was known for adapting militant methods of protest and holding often-xenophobic sentiments regarding mainland China, unorthodox at its time. The group had its first LegCo member in 2016 in Cheng Chung-tai who maintained an unconventional personality inside the chamber, oftentimes deliberately.

Demosisto 香港眾志 (L)

It was founded by the core members of Scholarism and the HKFS, the two student activist groups that played an instrumental role during the Occupy protests of 2014. Notable members include Joshua Wong, Agnes Chow and Nathan Law, the city’s leading student leaders during the 2014 protests who were all well known internationally. Advocating “democratic self-determination,” the latter won a legislative seat in 2016 at the age of 23, becoming the youngest ever legislator in Hong Kong‘s history. Law’s tenure lasted for only nine months as he was ruled disqualified after his  manners at the oath-taking ceremony. Agnes Chow planned to run in the subsequent HKI by-election in March 2018 to replace him, but was disqualified as well. 

Law planned to run again this time; however, with the NSL looming near, the group disbanded itself on June 30, 2020 following its enactment. Law  left for London the same day and instead endorsed Tiffany Yuen, a former member of the group until 2018.
